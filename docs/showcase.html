The #OpenFlaw Showcase

Real-world examples of people, software, and organizations that embody these principles‚Äîshipping fast, disclosing flaws, refusing to let imperfections halt progress.

Elon Musk

In February 2025, during DOGE work at the White House, Musk admitted: "Some of the things that I say will be incorrect and should be corrected. Nobody's going to bat 1,000. We will make mistakes, but we'll act quickly to correct any mistakes."

His willingness to ship despite known flaws creates velocity. SpaceX landed rockets after multiple Falcon 1 failures‚Äîeach failure was public, documented, and followed by rapid iteration.

Richard Branson

Dropped out of school at 16. Failed multiple businesses before Virgin. His brand: "Do not be embarrassed by your failures, learn from them and start again." Each failure became a story of resilience.

Sam Altman

OpenAI's 2023 board crisis‚ÄîAltman was ousted, then reinstated. The entire messy process was public. Altman: "I learned a lot from this episode." They published post-mortems and kept shipping GPT-4o, o1, ChatGPT.

üíª Software (OpenClaw & Others)

OpenClaw (The Torchbearer)

An AI agent with root access to user machines‚Äîan inherent flaw. China's ministry warned about security risks. Security researchers found data exfiltration. The team shipped weekly anyway, documented tradeoffs in README, fixed bugs fast.

In 73 days (December 2025 ‚Äì February 2026), it went from weekend project to being acquired by OpenAI.

The flaw was the feature: shipping a powerful, dangerous tool and openly documenting why it was risky.

Linux Kernel

Linus Torvalds: "Security is important, but it's not more important than having a working system." Kernel vulnerabilities are public, sometimes left unfixed for years. "There's no such thing as a perfect kernel." Ships anyway.

Docker

Container privilege escalation vulnerabilities are documented. They warn: "Docker containers are not sandboxed." They document every known vulnerability publicly and ship anyway.

Basecamp (37signals)

Their book REWORK argues against long development cycles. They champion shipping "minimum viable products."

Tesla

Cars shipped with known limitations. "Full Self-Driving" includes warnings: "The system may do the wrong thing." Recalls handled as over-the-air updates‚Äîpublic and frequent.

Wikipedia

The encyclopedia anyone can edit. Flaws (errors, biases, vandalism) are documented. Yet it's one of humanity's most valuable information resources‚Äîbecause radical openness enables continuous correction.

The US Government (DOGE)

The Department of Government Efficiency publicly publishes findings, admits mistakes, and moves fast. Musk: "We will make mistakes, but we'll act quickly to correct any mistakes." #OpenFlaw in government.

The Pattern

  1. They ship anyway. Perfection is not the goal‚Äîutility is.
  2. They document the flaws. README files, public warnings, blog posts.
  3. They fix what they can. Prioritize high-impact, low-cost fixes.
  4. They don't let flaws stop them. The enemy of good is perfect.
